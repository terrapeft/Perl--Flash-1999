Julie Bowen

By Jessica Wallenfels
UltimateTV News

 Bet you didn't think anyone who majored in Italian Renaissance studies at Brown University could end up starring on a mystery-spy-thriller TV show, replete with guns, gadgets and high heels. That's Julie Bowen for you, a bundle of contradictions who plays Amanda Webb on the WB's new "Mod Squad"-esque "Three." When asked about "Three," Bowen describes: "It is about me wearing short shirts carrying big guns... oh, and there are two other guys."

As the calculating Amanda, Bowen plays "an orphaned man-hater who has decided to get back at her past by taking advantage of men now..." The government-employed con-woman also does some mean karate. Amanda is a gearhead as well as a drop-dead babe, lecturing authoritatively about "fractal interpolator conversations" "and sub-sonic mutilators." How does Bowen take to the rigors of series-regular-dom? "I'm a workaholic so doing series work gives me a opportunity to work my butt off," she grins.

It wasn't until after Bowen had spent a year in Florence, Italy, became fluent in Italian, and returned to Brown for her senior year that she landed the leading role in an independent film, "Five Spot Jewel." Though she'd grown up putting on plays in the basement with her sisters in Baltimore, MD, and even starred in Brown's productions of "Guys and Dolls," "Stage Door" and "Lemon Sky," 'Jewel' would be the consumation of Bowen's love of acting.

Somewhere in between graduating from Brown and being named one of Detour magazine's "30 Under 30" to watch, Bowen get plenty of chances to hone her craft. After earning professional stripes on a series of commercials, a recurring role on the daytime drama "Loving," and a guest starring role on "Class of '96," Bowen was cast in a film called "No Visible Bruises." The filmmaker, Ed Burns, would go on to independent notoriety as the director of "The Brothers McMullen" and "She's The One."

Bowen took her cue and moved to Los Angeles. Unlike the thousands of struggling actors who take years to earn a little part, Bowen immediately landed the lead in the Showtime movie "Runaway Daughters."

Adam Sandler would become the unlikely leading man in Bowen's first major studio film, "Happy Gilmore." Was Sandler below Bowen's high-falutin' mind? "He is really funny," she says emphatically, "And secretly one of the smartest people I've ever met."

"Multiplicity" would be Bowen's next move, (with Michael Keaton) preceeding a move back to TV as a series regular on "Extreme." More guest starring roles followed, for the shows "Party of Five" and "Strange Luck."

Showbiz works in mysterious ways. After "Multiplicity," Bowen dug into a tasty acting challenge that would, as Bowen, says ruefully "never see the light of day." "The Killing Club," a noir-ish story of three women who go on a killing spree, is the project Bowen calls her most artistically satisfying. "I liked the idea, and the mental exploration that went on when they killed somebody. Intellectual masturbation. "

Before finding a possible home in "Three," Bowen appeared in last summer's "American Werewolf in Paris." But "Three" definitely appeals to Bowen's sensibilities. Of what attracted her to the role, Bowen reports, "The opportunity to get to be one of the boys on an action show, but without having to sacrifice being a female. I definitely use my sexuality, but I am equal terms with these guys. " As for on-set relations, Bowen expresses nothing but love for her cohorts. "My castmates are very funny. I am the dullard on that set. They are so much fun and very funny."

Bowen currently resides in Los Angeles. An avid sportswoman, she enjoys spending time with her two dogs as well as skiing, swiming and skydiving.
